Logarithms

The inverse of exponentiation. Logarithm problems range in difficulty from the mundane to the sublime but appear nearly every year on the AMC 12 and AIME.

Prerequisites: This article will be most useful to those who are looking to extend their knowledge to more difficult contest math problems. Those who have no experience with logarithms should consider finding additional practice until they are comfortable with the properties described in the theory section.

What Are Logarithms?

Suppose we wish to isolate $x$ in the equation $a^x=b$. How would we do this?

Here's where logarithms come into play.

Logarithm

Given any number $a>0$ and $a\neq 1$, $$\log_ab=x \Longleftrightarrow a^x=b.$$ We refer to $x$ as the exponent, $a$ as the base, and $b$ as the argument.

For example $\log_216=4$ because $2^4=16$. Similarly, we also have $\log_5(\frac{1}{25})=-2$, and $\log_{\frac{1}{2}}(\frac{1}{8})=3$.

Note that $a^x=b$ is referred to as exponential form while $x=\log_ab$ is referred to as logarithmic form.

Unless stated otherwise, if a logarithm does not include a base, assume the base is 10. If the logarithm is written as "ln" instead of "log," assume the base is $e$.

Now that we've got a grasp on the basics, let's take a look at some fundamental rules. From now on, assume that all variables are positive for the rest of the theory section.

Addition
Addition

$\log_ab+\log_ac=\log_a(bc)$

Proof. By Theorem 1, we have

$\log_ab+\log_ac=x+y=\log_a(bc).$

Power Rule
Power Rule

$\log_a(b^n)=n\log_ab$

Proof. By Theorem 1, we have

$n\log_ab=\underbrace{\log_ab+\log_ab+...+\log_ab}_{n \text{ times}}=\log_a(b^n).$
Subtraction
Subtraction

$$\log_ab-\log_ac=\log_a\left(\frac{b}{c}\right)$$

Proof. Using Theorem 2 and then Theorem 1, we have

$$\log_ab-\log_ac=\log_ab+\log_a\left(\frac{1}{c} \right)=\log_a\left(\frac{b}{c}\right).$$
Change of Base
Change of Base

$$\frac{\log_ab}{\log_ac}=\log_cb$$

Proof. Let $\log_ab=x$, $\log_ac=y$, $\log_cb=z$. Then $a^x=b$, $a^y=c$, and $c^z=b$. We have

$$(a^y)^z=a^x\implies \frac{x}{y}=z \implies \frac{\log_ab}{\log_ac}=\log_cb.$$

Note that if we let $a=10$, we simply get

$$\frac{\log b}{\log c}=\log_cb.$$
Summary and Problem Solving Tips

The two most useful properties are the change of base formula and the conversion from exponential to logarithmic form. Generally, these are enough to derive many other properties.

The change of base formula can be useful in reverse, especially if you're able to rewrite several logarithms as fractions and then do some canceling.


AMC 12B 2021/9
What is the value of
$$\frac{\log_2 80}{\log_{40}2}-\frac{\log_2 160}{\log_{20}2}?$$
Walkthrough

Key: get rid of the fractions and then recognize that there is a convenient substitution.

  1. Show that $\dfrac{1}{\log_{a}b} = \log_ba$, and then apply it to our problem.
  2. Let $x=\log_220$, and then write everything in terms of $x$.
  3. Expand the resulting expression.

AIME II 2020/3
The value of $x$ that satisfies $\log_{2^x} 3^{20} = \log_{2^{x+3}} 3^{2020}$ can be written as $\dfrac{m}{n}$, where $m$ and $n$ are relatively prime positive integers. Find $m+n$.
Walkthrough

Key: get rid of the exponents in both the base and the argument.

  1. Show that $\log_{a^n}b^m=\dfrac{m}{n}\log_ab$ using the properties of exponents, and then apply it to our problem.
  2. Cross multiply, then solve the resulting linear equation.

AMC 12B 2013/22
Let $m>1$ and $n>1$ be integers. Suppose that the product of the solutions for $x$ of the equation
$$8(\log_n x)(\log_m x)-7\log_n x-6 \log_m x-2013 = 0$$
is the smallest possible integer. What is $m+n$?
Walkthrough

Key: look for a way to use Vieta's formulas.

  1. Rearrange the expression so that it is a quadratic with respect to $\log x$.
  2. Use Vieta's Formula and simplify the resulting expression.
    • Hint: If you try to use Vieta's to directly find the product of the possible values of $\log x$, you'll get something quite ugly. Instead, think about how you could use the addition property of logarithms to your advantage.
  3. Remember that $m$ and $n$ are both integers greater than $1$. This severely limits our options since this is an optimization problem. Analyze the exponents of the expression you're trying to minimize and make sure they come out to be integers.
