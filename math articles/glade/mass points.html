Mass Points

A problem-solving method that is especially useful for geometry questions involving ratios of lengths. Valuable for contests that emphasize speed, like the AMC and MATHCOUNTS, because it can help you save a lot of time. Related to barycentric coordinates and the physics concept of torque.

Prerequisites: Basic knowledge of algebra and properties of triangles. Some of the more advanced problems involve the angle bisector theorem, Stewart's Theorem, and Heron's Formula.

What Are Mass Points?
Mass Point

A mass point is of the form $aP$ where a is the mass of a point P. The mass of any mass point must be positive and real.

Since math problems generally only provide lengths of ratios of lengths, it is often up to the reader to assign a value for $a$.

Mass points are most commonly used for triangles, but they can be used for just about any polygon, since all polygons can be decomposed into triangles.

The idea of a mass point is based on the physics concept of torque, specifically Archimedes' Law of the Lever. This has to do with rotation and can be visualized as a seesaw, as we will see in the next toggle.

Here is a quick notation tip to avoid notation abuse: use lowercase letters for mass values and an uppercase equivalent to denote the corresponding point. This means that "$a$" could be the mass of a point labeled "$A$."

Archimedes' Law of the Lever and Centers of Mass

Now that we know what mass points are, let's take a look at why they're useful! But before we get started, it would be worthwhile to understand the motivation behind where mass points come from. Let's take a look at Archimedes' Law of the Lever.

Archimedes' Law of the Lever

Magnitudes are in equilibrium at distances reciprocally proportional to their weights.

Don't worry if that was confusing. To better understand this, consider the seesaw illustrated below. Different masses are placed on the two ends. In order to keep the seesaw in equilibrium, heavier masses must be placed closer to the fulcrum (the point that the seesaw is resting on) and lighter masses must be placed farther away. The product of each mass and its distance to the fulcrum must be the same.

In the above image, Archimedes' Law of the Lever states that $m_1d_1=m_2d_2$.

As with all of our other diagrams, this is only a sketch and is not to scale. The boxes are there for illustrative purposes and are supposed to represent mass points. In practice, more massive points on the seesaw would not take up more physical space. (After all, all points are technically the same size.) Thus, from a purely mathematical perspective, each distance $d_i$ should be the distance from each mass point to the fulcrum.

Center of Mass

Given a system of objects, the center of mass gives the average location of those objects with respect to each object's mass. Every system of mass points has a unique center of mass.

Think of it as a "balancing point." (In physics, we often treat large or irregularly shaped objects as having all of their mass located at their center of mass.) For example, if we have a system of two identical mass points, their center of mass will be located right in between them. On the contrary, if one of those points is heavier than the other, the center of mass will be located closer to the heavier point.

Mass Point Addition

Supoise we have mass points $aP$ and $bQ$. Let their center of mass be point $R$. Then $R$ has a mass of $a+b.$

Archimedes' Law of the Lever tells us that $$a(PR)=b(RQ),$$ so $R$ must be located such that $$\frac{a}{b}=\frac{RQ}{PR}.$$

Also, mass point addition is both commutative and associative.

We can also extend this property to include mass point subtraction. To do this, suppose that we were instead given two mass points $cP$ and $dR$, with $d>c$. We can then subtract them to find the mass of a third point $Q$, located on the other side of $R$ opposite $P$: $$dR-cP = (d-c)Q.$$ In other words, the center of mass of mass points $cP$ and ($d-c)Q$ is $dR$, where $R$ is located in between $P$ and $Q$.

Systems of More than Two Mass Points

We just established the idea of balancing a "seesaw" to find the center of mass of two mass points. But what if we've got three or four mass points? Or even more than that? Luckily, the mass points technique can easily be extended to systems of lots of points. For example, most basic mass points problems involve triangles, specifically cevians:

The Cevian

For a given triangle $ABC$, a cevian is a line segment from one vertex to the opposing side. The term "cevian" is very broad and can include angle bisectors, medians, perpendicular bisectors, etc. (Note that not all perpendicular bisectors are cevians because they don't necessarily go through one of the triangle's vertices.)

In the image below, $AD$ is a cevian of $\triangle ABC$.

When solving basic mass points problems, you'll often be given a triangle with a few cevians where you have to find a specific ratio of lengths. To do this, let the intersection of those cevians be the triangle's center of mass. Then you'll be able to treat each cevian as a "seesaw" and assign weights to the triangle's vertices accordingly.

Splitting Mass Points

Now we know how to assign mass points to triangles with cevians, but what if we're not dealing with cevians? What if we're dealing with lines that still intersect the triangle but don't go through any of its vertices? In this case, you're probably wondering about transversals:

The Transversal

A transversal is a line intersecting two distinct lines at two distinct points.

In the image below, $DE$ is a transversal because it intersects $AB$ and $AC$ at points $D$ and $E$.

To address these scenarios, use the following method:

Imagine that sides $AB$ and $AC$ are separate "seesaws" with fulcrums at $D$ and $E$. See how point $A$ is suddenly a part of two different seesaws? This means that $A$ will have a split mass.

Pretend that $A$ is made up of two points stuck together: $A_1$ and $A_2$. $A_1$ and $B$ constitute a set of mass points with $D$ as their center of mass, while $A_2$ and $C$ constitute a separate set of mass points with $E$ as their center of mass.

Now suppose that $A_1$ has a mass of $x$ and $A_2$ has a mass of $y$. Now we can just add those two mass points to find the mass of point $A$: $$xA_1+yA_2=(x+y)A,$$ so the mass of $A$ is just $x+y$.

Summary and Problem Solving Tips

Mass points problems often start with a diagram and some ratios of lengths. These ratios can help you determine what masses to assign to which points. After that, Archimedes' Law of the Lever can be used to figure out remaining ratios of lengths.

When dealing with cevians of a triangle, treat their intersection as the triangle's center of mass and use Archimedes' Law of the Lever on each cevian.

Consider assigning masses that are multiples of the given lengths or ratios of lengths to make your calculations easier.

When annotating diagrams, it can be useful to circle your mass values on your diagrams to avoid confusing them with lengths. We will do this for the example problems.


MyMathcounts AMC 10 Preparation Book V4
In $\triangle ABC$, point $D$ is the midpoint of $BC$ and point $E$ lies on $AC$. $AD$ and $BE$ intersect at $G$. If $\dfrac{AC}{CE} = 3$, what is $\dfrac{AG}{GD}$?
Solution

Important note: from here on, we'll denote all points with capital letters and their respective masses with their lowercase equivalents.

Let $a=1$ for simplicity. We're given that $\dfrac{AC}{CE}=3$. This means that $\dfrac{AE}{CE}=2$ because $AE+EC=AC$. Using the properties of mass points, we have that $$AE\cdot a = EC \cdot c \Rightarrow \frac{AE}{EC}=\frac{c}{a} \Rightarrow c = a\left(\dfrac{AE}{EC}\right)$$ which means that $c=1(2)=2$.

We also know that $D$ is the midpoint of $BC$, so $\dfrac{BD}{DC}=1$. This means that $b=c$, so $b=2$. Since $D$ lies between $B$ and $C$ and we already know that $b=c=2$, we have that $$d = b+c = 4.$$ We're almost done. We use Archimedes' Law of the Lever one more time: $$\frac{AG}{GD}=\frac{d}{a}=\frac{4}{1}=\boxed{4}.$$

AMC 10B 2013/16
In triangle $ABC$, medians $AD$ and $CE$ intersect at $P$, $PE=1.5$, $PD=2$, and $DE=2.5$. What is the area of $AEDC$?
Solution

As with the previous example, we let $a=1$. We know that $AE=EB$ because $CE$ is a median. This means that $b=1$ as well.

We follow similar steps to show that $c=1$ because $AD$ is also a median. It follows that $$d=e=1+1=2$$ because of mass point addition.

Now we consider the relationship between $AP$ and $PD$. This problem is simple enough that we don't need to compare ratios algebraically, so instead we'll use the seesaw analogy from the theory section. Remember that the mass of each point is inversely proportional to its distance from the fulcrum.

Let $P$ be the fulcrum of "seesaws" $AD$ and $CE$. Since $a$ is half the mass of $d$, $a$ must be twice as far away from the fulcrum. This means that $AP=4$ because we're given that $PD=2$.

Likewise, we see that $E$ must be half as far away from the fulcrum as $C$ because $E$ has twice the mass of $C$. This means that $CP=3$ because $EP=1.5$.

Almost there. Note that $\triangle DPE$ is just a $3-4-5$ right triangle scaled down by one-half, where $\angle DPE = 90^\circ$. This means that the diagonals of $AEDC$ are perpendicular. Therefore, its area is $$\frac{AD \cdot CE}{2} = \frac{4.5(6)}{2}=\boxed{13.5}.$$ (The area of $AECD$ can also be found by adding up areas of the four right triangles comprising it.)

AIME I 2009/5
Triangle $ABC$ has $AC = 450$ and $BC = 300$. Points $K$ and $L$ are located on $\overline{AC}$ and $\overline{AB}$ respectively so that $AK = CK$, and $\overline{CL}$ is the angle bisector of angle $C$. Let $P$ be the point of intersection of $\overline{BK}$ and $\overline{CL}$, and let $M$ be the point on line $BK$ for which $K$ is the midpoint of $\overline{PM}$. If $AM = 180$, find $LP$.
Solution

We start by labeling our diagram with the given information. We know that $AC=450$ and $K$ is its midpoint, so $AK=CK=225$. We're also given that $CL$ bisects $\angle C$, so we can use the angle bisector theorem on $\triangle ABC$: $$\frac{BL}{AL}=\frac{BC}{AC}\Rightarrow\frac{BL}{AL}=\frac{300}{450}\Rightarrow 3BL=2AL$$

This suggests that we let $a=2$ and $b=3$ so that our mass point ratios will involve integers. We also have $c=2$ because $AK=CK$.

From mass point addition, we see that $k=a+c=4$, $p=k+b=7$, and $l=a+b=5$.

Since $L$ has $5/2$ the mass of $C$, it must be $2/5$ as far from $P$. In other words, $LP=\dfrac25 CP$. If we can just find $CP$, we'll be good to go.

We're given that $MK=KP$ and $AK=CK$ and we know that vertical angles $AKM$ and $CKP$ are equal, so $\triangle AKM \cong \triangle CKP.$ This means that $AM=CP$. We're given that $AM = 180$. Thus, we have $$LP=\frac25CP=\frac25(180)=\boxed{72}.$$
