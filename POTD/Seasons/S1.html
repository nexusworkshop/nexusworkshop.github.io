May 31, 2021 (HMMT February Team Round 2013/1)

Let $a$ and $b$ be real numbers such that $ \frac {ab}{a^2 + b^2} = \frac {1}{4} $. Find all possible values of $ \frac {|a^2-b^2|}{a^2+b^2} $.

Solution

We have \[ \frac{\sqrt{(|a^2-b^2|)^2}}{a^2+b^2} = \frac{\sqrt{(a+b)^2(a-b)^2}}{a^2+b^2}. \] From the relation $a^2+b^2 = 4ab$, we can deduce $(a+b)^2= 6a$ and $(a-b)^2= 2a$. Plugging in gives \[\frac{\sqrt{12}ab}{4ab} = \boxed{\frac{\sqrt{3}}2},\] which works. One example is $(a,b)=(1, 2\pm \sqrt{3})$. $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

May 28, 2021 and May 29, 2021 (AIME I 2016/12)

Find the least positive integer $m$ such that $m^2 - m + 11$ is a product of at least four not necessarily distinct primes.

Solution

Notice that $2 \mid m(m-1)$ so $2\nmid m^2-m+11$. Thus, \[p\mid m^2-m+11 \implies p\mid 4m^2-4m+44=(2m-1)^2+43\] so $(2m-1)^2\equiv -43\pmod{p}$. From here, we can plug in $p=3,5,7$, all of which fail so all of the prime factors must be $11$ or larger.

Let $m^2 - m + 11 = pqrs$ for primes $p, q, r, s\ge11$. Since we are looking for the least positive integer, let $(p,q,r,s)=(11,11,11,11)$. Multiplying by $4$ and completing the square gives \[(2m-1)^2=4\cdot 11^4-43.\] However we get a contradiction: \[(2\cdot 11^2-1)^2=4\cdot 11^4-4\cdot 11^2+1 < 4\cdot 11^4-43<(2\cdot 11^2)^2,\] which means that there lies a perfect square in between two consecutive perfect squares. Thus, $$\begin{align*} m^2-m+11\ge 11^3\cdot 13 & \implies (m-132)(m+131)\ge0 \\ & \implies m \ge 132 \end{align*}$$ Since $132^2 - 132 + 11 = 11^3 \cdot 13$, $m=\boxed{132}$. $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

May 27, 2021 and May 28, 2021 (AMC 12A 2017/23)

For certain real numbers $a$, $b$, and $c$, the polynomial\[g(x) = x^3 + ax^2 + x + 10\]has three distinct roots, and each root of $g(x)$ is also a root of the polynomial\[f(x) = x^4 + x^3 + bx^2 + 100x + c.\]What is $f(1)$?

Solution

Notice that we have \[f(x) = C(x-k)g(x)\] for some number $k$. Also, we can see that $C=1$ because the coefficient of $x^4$ in $f(x)$ is $1$. Thus, \[x^4+x^3+bx^2+100x+c=(x-k)(x^3+ax^2+x+10)\] Equating coefficients gives \[a-k=1,1-ak=b,10-k=100,-10k=c\] which we can solve to find $k=-90$ and $a=-89$. Our answer is \[f(1)=(1+90)g(1)=91(1-89+1+10)=(91)(-77)=\boxed{-7007}.\] $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

May 25, 2021 and May 26, 2021 (CMIMC Combinatorics 2017/1)

Robert colors each square in an empty 3 by 3 grid either red or green. Find the number of colorings such that no row or column contains more than one green square.

Solution
Let $k$ be the number of green squares. By Pigeonhole, $0\le k \le 3$. Now we do casework.
  • $k=0$. There is only 1 coloring, of all red squares.
  • $k=1$. There is 9 colorings because you can place the one green square in any of 9 squares.
  • $k=2$. There is $\frac{1}{2}(9 \cdot 4)=18$ because there are only four choices left for the second green square after placing the first green square, and we divide by 2 because order does not matter.
  • $k=3$. There are $\frac{1}{6}(9 \cdot 4 \cdot 1)=6$ colorings. There are 9 choices for the first green square, 4 for the second green square, and 1 for the last green square. We divide by 6 because order does not matter.
Adding up our cases gives $1+9+18+6=\boxed{34}$. $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$
May 24, 2021 (PUMAC Algebra B 2010/1)

Let the operation $\bigstar$ be defined by $x\bigstar y=y^x-xy$. Calculate $(3\bigstar4)-(4\bigstar3)$.

Solution
We have $$\begin{align*} (3\bigstar4)-(4\bigstar3) &=(4^3 - 3\cdot 4) - (3^4 - 4\cdot 3) \\ &= (64 - 12) - (81 - 12) \\ &= \boxed{-17 } \end{align*}$$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$
